Why molecules repel each other¶

It is often claimed that molecules bounce off each other because they have negative charges on the outside that repel each other. This claim appears a million places, but I'll pick on a few:

Clayden et al, "Organic Chemistry", 2nd edition, p108:

Molecules are coated with a layer of electrons which occupy bonding and maybe non- bonding orbitals. As a result the surface of each molecule is negatively charged and by and large molecules repel each other.

More ironically, Natalie Angier in "The Canon" writes about public understanding of science:

A fair question to interject here is: Need we do anything at all? Does it matter if the great majority of people know little or nothing about science or the scientific mindset? If the average Joe or Sophie doesn't know the name of the closest star (the sun), or whether tomatoes have genes (they do), or why your hand can't go through a tabletop (because the electrons in each repel each other), what difference does it make?

I don't think this explanation is correct.

When you bring molecules closer together, here's what happens to the components of the energy:

  • Electron kinetic energy increases slightly
  • Electron-electron Coulomb energy increases slightly, but not as much as the electron kinetic energy
  • Electron-nucleon Coulomb energy decreases substantially but not unboundedly
  • Nucleon-nucleon Coulomb energy increases substantially and becomes unboundedly large (if you move the atoms literally right on top of each other).

If you have to explain the repulsion in terms of only one of these factors, I think that the nucleon-nucleon repulsion is the best choice. Electron KE is the next best explanation, because it's a larger factor than electron-electron repulsion, and because it is kind of more fundamental factor determining the length scale of chemistry.

So if you have to give a simple explanation of why molecules repel each other, I think the best one is "Nuclei repel each other. Sometimes, atoms can bond, which allows electrons to get into sufficiently energetically favorable states that it's energetically 'worth it' for the nuclei to be near each other. But if this doesn't happen, then as your molecules get closer, even though electronic energy monotonically decreases, the nuclear repulsion gets stronger and stronger and causes the molecules to bounce off each other."

A slightly longer true explanation is "Nuclei repel each other. When far away, they are shielded by their electrons. As they get very close, though, the electrons no longer shield them as much, and so they end up repelling strongly. The reason that electrons no longer shield nuclei properly at short ranges is kind of hard to explain because it involves the quantum mechanics of electrons, but a rough heuristic explanation is 'electron states where the electrons are highly localized are high energy, which is exacerbated by the Pauli exclusion principle forcing electrons to occupy different states (because they have to occupy less good highly-localized states, which are even higher energy)'.

Here are some relevant arguments and intuitions.

  • Actual calculations. Below, I calculate total energy as a function of distance for the following systems: two H2 molecules, two He atoms, and one NH3 and one BH3. For each, you'll observe that as the molecules get closer, the electron energy monotonically decreases, and the nuclear energy monotonically increases, adding up to a net repulsion at short ranges.
  • He2 vs Be. Suppose I have two He atoms and I move their nuclei on top of each other. From the perspective of the electrons, this is the same as them being the electrons of a Be atom. Be atoms have a ground state energy more than 2x lower than He atoms. This is a specific instance of the general statement that as molecules move closer together, electron energy decreases.
  • Analogy to the shell theorem. It's true that when I have two molecules near each other, their electrons are nearby each other and repelling each other. But their electrons are also attracted to the nuclei of the other molecule, and in fact the electrons of the first molecule are closer to the nuclei of the other molecule than they are to around half of the second molecule's electron. This is reminiscent of the shell theorem, which states that you can treat a spherically symmetric distribution of charge as a point charge at the center of the distribution. That theorem doesn't totally apply here, because we have no particular guarantee of spherical symmetry, but it points to why the "the electrons repel each other" intuition is wrong.

In the rest of this notebook, I demonstrate this with a variety of calculations. The casual reader might want to only look at the plots and ignore the code. All distances are in Angstroms and all energies are in Hartrees.

# Various utility functions

def energy_components(mf):
    # Given an optimized HF state, determine the different components of the energy.
    # This function is based on energy_elec in PySCF's scf.hf module.
    dm = mf.make_rdm1()

    ke_op = mf.mol.intor('int1e_kin')
    ke = np.einsum('ij,ji->', ke_op, dm)
    
    nuc_op = mf.mol.intor('int1e_nuc')
    nuc = np.einsum('ij,ji->', nuc_op, dm)
    
    vhf = mf.get_veff(mf.mol, dm)
    e_coul = np.einsum('ij,ji->', vhf, dm) * .5
    # returns: electron KE, electron-nuclear attraction, electron-electron repulsion,
    # nucleon-nucleon repulsion
    return (ke, nuc, e_coul, mf.energy_nuc())

def do_hf(mol_str, basis="ccpvdz"):
    return scf.RHF(gto.M(atom = mol_str, basis = basis, verbose=0)).run()

Electron states around Be vs He2¶

Below, I calculate the components of the energy for two He atoms at a distance of 0.02Å, which is ridiculously close. (If you put them literally on top of each other, you get a divide-by-zero error while calculating nucleon-nucleon repulsion.) The first three numbers are the electron KE, nuclear PE, and electron-electron PE. Note that they are very similar. The final number is the nucleon-nucleon repulsion energy, which is obviously extremely high for the He2 and zero for the Be.

def ammonia_at(z):
    # Returns a string describing an ammonia molecule with the N at (0,0,z),
    # oriented with H below N.
    
    # This is an approximation of the optimal geometry of NH3, because I
    # didn't want to figure out how to convert "102 pm bonds at 109 degrees"
    # into Cartesian coordinates.
    return """N 0 0 {n_z}; H 0.9 0 {h_z}; 
            H -0.45 0.78 {h_z}; H -0.45 -0.78 {h_z}""".format(n_z=z, h_z=z - 0.1)
